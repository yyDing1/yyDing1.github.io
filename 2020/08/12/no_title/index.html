hdu5976

main idea

Now you want to divede a number $x$ into some(including one) different numbers which add up to $x$, in order to maximize the product of these numbers. ($eg: 4 = 1 + 3, 4 = 4, answer(4) = max(1 * 3, 4) = 4)$

Solution

Assume result to be $a_1, a_2, a_3, …, a_p$ in increasing order.

Some conclusion(on the contition that $x > 4$)

  1. $a_1 < 4$
  2. for i from 2 to p, $a_{i} - a_{i - 1} \leq 2$, and there exists at most one i that $a_{i} - a_{i - 1} = 2$(maybe don’t exist)

Here is the simple proof:

The general idea of the proof is to prove the contrary condition is not optimal.

  1. Imagine that $a_1 \geq 4$. exists. We can replace $a_1$ with $2, a_1 - 2$, then the contribution of $a_1$ increases $(2(a_1 - 2) < a_1)$. No conditions that $a_1 \geq 4$ are optimal. So $a_1 < 4$.
  2. First if there exists i that satisfies $a_{i} - a_{i - 1} > 2$, then we can replace $a_i, a_{i - 1}$ with $a_i - 1, a_{i - 1} + 1$, and it’s better. In addition, if there exists two position $i, j(i < j)$ that satisfies that $a_{i} - a_{i - 1} = 2, a_{j} - a_{j - 1} = 2$, we can replace $a_{i - 1}, a_j$ with $a_{i - 1} + 1, a_{j} - 1$. They are neighter optimal. Proposition 2 gets proved.

In summary, array $a$ appears like $2, 3, 4, …, m - 1, m + 1, m + 2, …, p$.

Other details:

  1. the process can be viewed as:
    • find a array $2, 3, 4, …, n$ that the sum is just smaller than or equal to x.
    • then elect the max some element, and adjust them to make the sum equal to x.
  2. prefix product combined with inverse can simplify every query of $A_{n}^{m}$ to $O(log(1e9)) \approx O(30)$
  3. binary search makes it quick to get n(mentioned in datail 1). $O(log(1e9))\approx O(30)$
